# Are Developing Countries Tackling the Learning Crisis With What Works?

In a recent blog we outlined the extent to which two major donors, FCDO and the World Bank, mention the "smart buys" from the Global Education Evidence Advisory Panel (GEEAP) report in their project documentation. But what about national governments themselves?

Here we present a new analysis of more than 1,000 education-planning documents from more than 200 countries and territories spanning three decades, from 2000 to 2030. From the UNESCO Planipolis website we identified `820` national education plans. For this first analysis, we focus on the `528` plans that were available in English, covering `101` countries.

We then searched these plans for mentions of the eight specific GEEAP smart buys: `structured pedagogy`, `targeted instruction`, `specific information about the costs, benefits, and quality of education`, `early child stimulation`, `preschool`, `reducing travel time`, `merit scholarships`, and `mass deworming`. We did this using a retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) approach: first applying a broad search to identify potentially relevant passages, and then using an LLM to review the surrounding context and determine whether the content genuinely corresponds to a GEEAP smart buy, even when different terminology is used.

Only one document references the GEEAP smart buys project outright: Rwanda's `2024-28` Foundational Learning Strategy. But because our approach looks for the broader concepts behind each smart buy, we find that these ideas are consistently mentioned at least once in `40-80` percent of LMIC plans over the last 25 years. That suggests governments have often been using these kinds of cost-effective interventions, even when they are not using the GEEAP label. Targeted instruction and structured pedagogy, the two interventions most strongly associated with GEEAP in recent years, have both seen a steady increase in mentions over the last 20 years, but there is no obvious jump since the report itself.

Of the three flagship targeted instruction programmes being implemented by TaRL Africa, Zambia has a national programme that has also been rigorously evaluated, Côte d'Ivoire has a national programme, and Nigeria has state-level programmes. Zambia is captured in our broader smart-buy search using the RAG method. Côte d'Ivoire is not in the English-language database because it has no English plans, and we found no evidence of targeted instruction being mentioned in the recent Nigeria plans. Meanwhile, our search found none of the `14` other countries partnered with TaRL Africa mentioning targeted instruction in the English-language database.

## If Not Smart Buys, Then What Are Countries Talking About?

Aside from the occasional mention of smart buys or related concepts, what else are these plans talking about? To find out, we ran an open-ended topic model on the same corpus to identify the main clusters. The most common topic in low- and middle-income countries is access, appearing in `25` percent of plans, closely followed by education in emergencies. The biggest divergence between high-income and low-income countries is around higher education, which is much more common in richer countries, and digital learning.

Finally, the last time we looked at national government statements on education, at the `2022` Transforming Education Summit, we looked specifically at some of the neglected topics our team at CGD works on, including violence in schools, school meals, lead exposure, the global learning crisis, and foundational literacy and numeracy. Searching again for similar phrases in national plans, we find that school meals and school violence continue to be priorities, being mentioned in about half of documents in the last decade.

It is encouraging to see governments increasingly mentioning both the learning crisis and foundational learning since `2017`.

Ultimately, context matters. Not every country should have mass deworming, because not every country has a major worm burden. Likewise, not all of the other smart buys will be relevant in every country. But if only around a third of plans mention some of these interventions, is that still too low?
